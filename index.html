The Gamification of Learning: a Meta-analysis

Michael Sailer · Lisa Homner
Educational Psychology Review (2020) 32:77–112 | Published: 15 August 2019 DOI: 10.1007/s10648-019-09498-w
📘 Executive Summary

This landmark meta-analysis systematically synthesizes 38 primary studies (2013–2017) to examine whether gamification — the use of game design elements in non-game contexts — enhances cognitive, motivational, and behavioral learning outcomes. Using random-effects models, Sailer & Homner provide robust evidence that gamification yields small but significant positive effects across all three outcome domains. However, effects on motivation and behavior are less stable under rigorous methodological scrutiny. The study further investigates conceptual moderators (game fiction, social interaction) and contextual factors (duration, research setting, methodology), revealing that competitive-collaborative designs and game fiction are particularly effective for behavioral outcomes, with implications for instructional design.

Interpretation: Gamification significantly outperforms conventional instruction, with strongest effects on cognitive learning (conceptual & application knowledge). Effects on motivation and behavior are small but meaningful, yet caution is warranted: after restricting to high‑methodological‑rigor studies, motivational and behavioral effects became non‑significant, while cognitive effects remained stable (g = 0.42, p < .01).

🔍 What Works? Key Moderator Findings
🎲 Inclusion of Game Fiction
  • Behavioral outcomes: significant moderating effect (Q(1) = 5.45, p < .05). Gamification with game fiction (narrative context, avatars, story world) yielded g = 0.41** [0.31, 0.51], while without fiction g = 0.10 [–0.14, 0.34].
  • Cognitive & motivational outcomes: no significant difference between including vs. excluding game fiction. Possible explanation: design quality variance or that fiction mainly influences immediate task engagement/process data (most behavioral measures collected during intervention).
🤝 Social Interaction: Competition & Collaboration
  • Behavioral learning: significant moderation (Q(1) = 5.45 for game fiction? Wait – for social interaction: Q(2) = 5.45? Actually from Table 4: social interaction Q = 10.94*, p < .05). Competitive-collaborative designs (e.g., team competition) produced a large effect g = 0.70** [0.41, 0.99], whereas mere competition yielded g = 0.12 (non-significant) and no interaction g = –0.06.
  • Motivational outcomes (high-rigor subsplit): competitive-collaborative again outperformed pure competition (g = 0.61** vs. g = –0.09). Mere competition may thwart relatedness if perceived as destructive; combining competition with collaboration satisfies competence and relatedness needs.
  • Practical takeaway: design gamified learning that embeds team-based collaboration + inter-team competition rather than individual leaderboards alone.
⏱️ Period of Time (Motivation specific)

Interventions lasting half a year or less (but >1 month) showed a medium-sized effect on motivational outcomes (g = 0.56**), while very short interventions (≤1 day) were non-significant. This suggests that motivational benefits may require longer exposure, yet the endurance of effects beyond the intervention remains unclear (lack of delayed posttests).

🏫 Research Context

Cognitive outcomes were significantly larger in school settings compared to higher education or informal settings (p < .01). However, school studies more often used passive control groups, which might inflate effects.

🧪 Methodological rigor matters

✔️ Randomization: Quasi-experimental studies reported larger motivational effects than true experiments (g = 0.44 vs. 0.07), indicating possible bias. For cognitive outcomes, effect remained robust across designs.
✔️ High-rigor subsplits (only experimental or pre-post quasi-experimental designs):
   • Cognitive: still significant (g = 0.42, k = 9, fail-safe N = 51)
   • Motivational: non-significant (g = 0.22, p = .20)
   • Behavioral: non-significant (g = 0.27, p = .22)
➜ Thus, while gamification shows promise, current evidence for motivational and behavioral benefits is less robust when only high-quality studies are considered. More rigorous primary research is urgently needed.

💡 Theoretical & Practical Implications

🔬 Theory-driven insights: The study aligns with Landers’ theory of gamified learning (indirect effect via attitudes/behaviors) and Self-Determination Theory (competence, autonomy, relatedness). However, mechanisms are still underspecified — especially how game elements trigger high-quality (constructive/interactive) learning activities.

🎮 For practitioners:
• To foster skills and performance (behavioral outcomes), embed game fiction (storylines, avatars) and competitive-collaborative structures (e.g., teams competing against each other).
• Avoid pure competition without collaboration — it may backfire motivationally.
• Gamification is effective for knowledge acquisition (cognitive), but simple badges/points alone are insufficient; design must link game elements to meaningful learning behaviors.
• Consider longer-term interventions (several weeks/months) to see motivational benefits.

📚 Future research needs: • High-quality experimental designs with pretest controls.
• Investigate learner characteristics (skill levels, player types, perceptions).
• Use process data (log files, learning analytics) to understand how learners actually interact with gamified elements.
• Include delayed posttests to evaluate endurance of effects.

📊 Publication Bias & Heterogeneity

Funnel plots and selection models indicated no severe publication bias; fail-safe Ns were robust for all three summary effects (all > 5n+10). However, substantial heterogeneity (I² = 51–75% across outcomes) remained unexplained even after including several moderators. This suggests that other moderators (e.g., specific game element design, individual differences, feedback type, implementation fidelity) likely influence gamification’s effectiveness.

Notably, behavioral outcomes were predominantly measured during the intervention (e.g., task completion, Lego car assembly), making it difficult to separate learning from immediate engagement. Future studies should assess behavioral performance both during and after gamification to avoid confounds.

⚠️ Key Limitations (Recognized by Authors)
  • Small sample sizes for behavioral outcomes (k = 9) and subgroup analyses → low statistical power; non-significant findings may reflect Type II error.
  • High heterogeneity and wide CIs for many moderator levels.
  • Lack of uniformity in outcome measures, and many studies used self-developed instruments with unknown psychometric quality.
  • Most primary studies did not report learner perceptions, skill levels, or actual engagement with game elements.
  • “Garbage in, garbage out” risk: low methodological rigor in part of the primary literature limits causal inferences.
✅ Conclusion: Does Gamification Work for Learning?

“Yes, gamification can be effective for learning, but the ‘how’ remains partly unresolved — especially for cognitive outcomes. While overall significant effects exist, motivational and behavioral benefits depend heavily on design features (competitive-collaboration, game fiction) and methodological quality. Gamification is not a magic bullet; it requires theory-driven design, rigorous evaluation, and alignment with learning processes.”
— Adapted from Sailer & Homner (2020)
